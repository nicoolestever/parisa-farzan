The Problem at the Center

Everything circles the same wound.

Parisa keeps ending up inside systems designed to respond to disaster. Medicine. Tactical work. The Hunter Society. The emergency room again. Different language, same architecture: pressure, hierarchy, triage, collateral.

The tragedy is not that she keeps choosing the wrong field. The tragedy is that every field finds a way to ask the same thing from her — compromise your conscience for efficiency, for secrecy, for control, for the greater good.

She keeps refusing at the exact point refusal becomes expensive.

Which is why she loses institutions and keeps people.

Former trainees still call. Former allies still test the boundaries. The Society still wants her usefulness without having to answer for what it did. And Parisa, against all common sense, still shows up when a bad night gets worse.

The Streets

1995–2000 · United States · tw: child neglect / abandonment, homelessness, hunger / food insecurity, child endangerment, survival theft, emotional deprivation, implied abuse / predatory adults

+

the first thing parisa learns is that hunger doesn't kill you as fast as people think it does.

it hurts. that's true. it hollows you out and settles into your bones until it feels less like a feeling and more like a second body living inside your own. sometimes it climbs into her throat and makes swallowing difficult even when there is nothing to swallow. sometimes it sharpens everything around her—the smell of grease drifting out of a diner kitchen, the rustle of paper bags in strangers' hands, the wet sweetness of fruit from a market stall half a block away. but it doesn't kill you. not quickly. the body is meaner than that. more stubborn. it keeps going. it keeps you going. it waits to see what you'll do next.

she is eight. maybe nine.

it is hard to tell.

time doesn't move properly when no one is keeping it for you. birthdays stop mattering when there is nobody to mark them, no candles, no badly wrapped gifts, no one saying look at that, you made it another year. eventually she stops counting altogether. age becomes something other children have—something written down on school forms and spoken aloud when adults ask questions.

what matters instead is knowing which streets are safer in the afternoon and which ones go bad as soon as the light changes. what matters is knowing which corner stores are careless, which managers watch the register instead of the back door, which church basements leave soup out too long after service, which adults will pretend not to see a skinny child slipping past them and which ones will smile first, then decide what they can take.

she learns early that hands matter more than faces.

faces lie. faces soften. faces perform. hands don't.

hands tell you whether someone is gentle or rough, tired or cruel, careless or fast. hands tell you when someone is about to grab. hands tell you when to move before the rest of the body has even decided what it wants. she becomes good at watching them. good enough that by the time someone reaches for her, she is already gone.

there is a park she goes to sometimes, though parks are a risk. too open. too many eyes. too many places to get cornered with nowhere useful to run. but one afternoon she ends up there anyway, half-dozing on a bench in the shade, empty enough inside that even moving feels like a bad idea.

that is when she sees the girl.

the girl is maybe her age. maybe a year older. clean-faced, cross-legged, sitting on the other end of the bench with an apple in both hands like it's the most ordinary thing in the world. she eats slowly. thoughtfully. not the way hungry people eat. not the way parisa eats whenever she gets lucky enough to find food. the girl takes careful bites, chewing like she has all the time in the world, like she has never once had to think about whether this might be the only thing she gets that day.

parisa stares too long.

the girl notices.

instead of getting weird about it, instead of clutching the apple tighter or calling for an adult, she just breaks it in half and holds one piece out.

just like that.

no questions. no pity. no why are you alone, no where are your parents, no careful adult voice trying to coax a story out of her.

just here.

you look like you need this more than i do.

parisa takes it.

she eats slowly on purpose, trying to match the girl's pace, trying to make the moment last longer than it has any right to. for ten minutes, maybe less, the world loosens around the edges. hunger quiets. the bench becomes something almost safe. they sit there side by side without really talking, and for one impossible little stretch of time parisa is not entirely alone inside her own skin.

then somebody calls the girl's name.

a woman—mother, probably—waves from the path, and the girl hops up and goes without hesitation, all bright movement and certainty, like of course someone came back for her. of course someone knew where she was. of course someone would.

parisa is left holding the apple core.

something ugly and dangerous opens in her chest.

want.

not for food this time.

for the girl to come back. for someone to look at her like that again—without fear, without disgust, without calculation. for the possibility that maybe people can hand you something without first deciding what they'll take in return.

and she knows immediately that this is a problem.

wanting things gets you hurt.

wanting people gets you ruined.

she never goes back to that park.

not because she's afraid of the girl, but because she's afraid of what it means that ten minutes on a bench could still reach something inside her that the rest of the world hasn't already starved to death.

so she learns to be invisible instead.

she makes herself small and quiet and forgettable. she figures out how to move through crowds like she belongs there and therefore doesn't need to be seen. she steals food in pieces—a bruised banana, half a sandwich, crackers from a vending machine refill left unattended too long. she learns the rhythm of a city by what it drops. she learns how long people will glance at a child before deciding not to get involved.

invisibility becomes a skill. then a habit. then something close to a religion.

if you don't take up space, people stop seeing you.

if they stop seeing you, they can't decide to hurt you.

there is a shelter run out of a church basement for a while. not a good one, not a permanent one, just thin mats on concrete and watery soup ladled into paper bowls by tired people trying their best. the woman who runs it is named park. she has lined hands, practical shoes, and the kind of face that looks permanently exhausted in a way parisa instinctively trusts more than cheerfulness.

ms. park does not ask questions.

that is what makes her dangerous.

that is also what makes her kind.

she lets parisa come in when the nights get mean enough that the cold stops feeling like weather and starts feeling personal. she hands her soup. a blanket once. space on the floor near a radiator that only works when it feels like it. she never presses for a name, never asks where she's been, never reaches unless parisa is too sick or too frozen to object.

one night she finds parisa shaking under a stairwell and brings her inside without a word. wraps her in a blanket that smells like detergent and dust and other people's homes. sits nearby until the shaking stops. doesn't pry. doesn't offer speeches. just stays.

it is unbearable.

because this, too, feels like wanting.

the apple girl had been a bright little miracle. ms. park is worse. ms. park is repetition. routine. proof that kindness might not be a one-time accident. proof that warmth can become habit if you let it.

and habit is the most dangerous thing of all.

because habit becomes dependence.

dependence becomes grief.

people leave.

some because they want to. some because they have to. some because the world takes them before they get a choice.

either way, they leave.

parisa understands that before she has the language for it.

so she makes one of the first real decisions of her life: she stops going.

she stops letting herself get warm. stops letting herself get used to soup, blankets, eye contact, the possibility of a place where people might notice if she doesn't come back. she cuts herself off before the wanting can settle too deeply. before she can start imagining a future that includes anything beyond the next day and the day after that.

instead she gets harder.

colder.

more efficient.

by twelve, maybe thirteen, she has taught herself things other children should never need to know. how to slip a lock with the right pressure and enough patience. how to move through hospital corridors like she belongs there. which waiting rooms stay crowded enough to disappear in and which ones empty out after midnight. how to read maintenance schedules pinned to staff boards. how to sit still long enough that people stop registering her as a person and start filing her under background.

she learns from watching. from library books. from scraps of old forums on public computers. from men fixing broken doors who never notice the girl in the corner memorizing what their hands are doing. information is everywhere if you know how to stay quiet long enough for it to reveal itself.

she learns where to hit if someone corners her.

she learns where to run.

she learns that fear is information and pain is information and both are only useful if you can stay still long enough to hear what they're telling you.

and through all of it, she keeps that apple in her head.

not the fruit itself. the moment.

the gesture.

the impossible simplicity of being seen and not immediately punished for it.

she carries it like contraband. like evidence. like proof that somewhere in the world there are people who give without taking first.

she does not believe that world belongs to her.

but she remembers it anyway.

years later, when people mistake her restraint for coldness, when they assume she was simply born difficult, they are only seeing the final shape and not the process that made it. they don't know that every wall in her was built deliberately. they don't know that she learned young that softness is expensive and wanting things is worse. they don't know that underneath all the discipline and sharp edges is the ghost of a child sitting on a park bench, eating half an apple as slowly as possible so the feeling of not being alone lasts just a little longer.

by the time parisa is old enough to understand what the streets have taken from her and what they gave back in return, the damage is already structural.

trust has hardened into something brittle.

hope has been filed down into practicality.

love, when she imagines it at all, feels less like a real possibility and more like a story invented to keep desperate people from giving up entirely.

but somewhere, buried deeper than she likes to look, she still remembers the taste of that apple.

still remembers what it felt like to be offered something without condition.

and maybe that is the cruelest part.

not that the world taught her to become hard.

that some small part of her learned, first, that she did not want to be.

Recruitment

2016 · United States · tw: institutional manipulation, recruitment into violence, medical stress / burnout, coercive systems, loss of autonomy, moral compromise

+

the recruiter finds parisa in the kind of hour that does not belong to decent people.

it is barely morning, though morning suggests something softer than this—the washed-out gray before sunrise, fluorescent light still clinging to the hospital windows, the city not yet fully awake enough to lie about itself. she has been on for fourteen hours. maybe fifteen. time loses structure in the emergency department when the worst night of someone else's life keeps restarting in different rooms.

her scrubs are clean only because she changed out of the ruined pair an hour ago. there is dried blood on one shoe she hasn't noticed yet. coffee gone cold in a paper cup beside the workstation. two sutures still fresh in the webbing of her hand from an agitated patient who got teeth involved. she has not sat down properly in six hours and isn't especially interested in starting now.

across the hall, a resident is trying and failing not to cry in the supply closet.

parisa signs off on a chart without looking up.

"if you're here to tell me my notes are too blunt," she says, "you can come back when i've had sleep and developed a conscience."

silence.

not offended silence. not confused silence.

measured silence.

that gets her attention.

she looks up.

the man standing at the edge of the nurse's station is unremarkable in the way only very deliberate people know how to be. neutral suit. neutral tie. face that would disappear in a crowd if it weren't for the eyes. the eyes are the problem. too alert. too patient. the kind of eyes that make parisa immediately start cataloging exits out of habit.

not administration, then.

worse.

"dr. farzan," he says.

she hates the way he says it. not disrespectful. not overly formal. just certain. like he already knows more about her than a stranger should.

"congratulations," she says flatly. "you can read a badge."

he does not smile.

that, more than anything, tells her this is not ordinary.

"can i have five minutes of your time?"

"can you?" she asks. "probably. should you? less clear."

he glances toward the trauma bay, toward the hallway, toward the places where chaos is always trying to reorganize itself into something more efficient and always failing. "somewhere private."

"that's a hard no."

"understood."

he says it too easily.

which means this isn't his first difficult conversation.

parisa leans back in her chair just enough to make it clear she is not moving, not following him, not doing him the favor of acting curious. if he wants this, he can have it under fluorescent lights with nurses eavesdropping and transport staff passing by.

"start talking."

he studies her for a second. not like a man looking at a woman. not like a superior appraising a subordinate. like someone checking numbers against expected output. verifying that a thing works the way the file said it would.

she knows that look.

it makes her want to break his nose.

"you stayed calm during a multi-car pileup with nine incoming traumas and one missing attending," he says.

"that was tuesday."

"you identified a tension pneumothorax before imaging."

"that was also tuesday."

"you made the call to crack a chest while two senior physicians argued procedure."

"they were wasting time."

he inclines his head once, like that is the answer he expected.

parisa's shoulders go still.

this man knows too much about tuesday.

"who are you?"

"a recruiter."

she stares at him.

then, because she's exhausted enough to be honest, she laughs.

it isn't a pleasant sound.

"for what," she asks, "the devil?"

something almost shifts in his expression. not humor. appreciation, maybe. the kind people reserve for useful tools that come pre-sharpened.

"for work that requires your particular skill set."

that is such a cliché that it would be funny if it weren't so irritating.

parisa picks up her coffee, remembers it's cold, sets it back down.

"let me guess. long hours, poor management, impossible expectations, and a high likelihood of bodily harm."

"yes."

that makes her pause.

"you could at least pretend to sell it better."

"you don't seem like the type to appreciate false advertising."

there it is.

the first honest thing he has said.

she narrows her eyes.

"still haven't told me who you are."

"someone who works with a government-adjacent program that recruits physicians for unstable environments."

parisa says nothing.

the phrase is clean. too clean. built to slide past scrutiny without technically lying. unstable environments. government-adjacent. physicians. she has heard people dress uglier things in prettier language before.

"combat medicine?" she asks.

"among other things."

"humanitarian?"

"occasionally."

"black site bullshit?"

that gets the faintest twitch at the corner of his mouth.

which is answer enough.

parisa looks away from him for the first time, out through the narrow wired window into the not-yet-morning, and feels something old and half-rotted shift under her ribs.

she should say no.

she should say absolutely not.

she has spent too many years clawing her way through medicine to hand herself over to a structure that sounds like a prettier version of violence with better funding. she knows what institutions do to people like her. they call it discipline when they mean obedience. they call it service when they mean ownership. they call you exceptional right up until the moment exceptional stops being convenient.

and yet.

there is something in her that has always gone still, not frightened but focused, when the room tips from difficult into impossible.

the emergency department taught her that.

the part of her that was there long before medicine just recognized itself in the language.

unstable environments.

no one ever phrases it that way unless they mean: places where the rules have already failed and someone still has to walk in anyway.

"why me?" she asks.

"because you are technically excellent," he says. "because pressure improves you instead of degrading you. because you don't freeze. because you are willing to make hard decisions quickly. and because every attending you've worked under either praises your judgment or complains that you do not wait long enough for permission."

"that last one sounds like a criticism."

"it's context."

"that's a fancy word for criticism."

he lets that pass.

"you've spent years learning how to save people after the damage is already done," he says. "we're offering work that begins earlier."

parisa goes quiet.

that is the line.

not patriotic duty. not service. not prestige.

earlier.

the possibility of intervening before the blood loss, before the panic, before the moment in the trauma bay where someone is already too broken and all she can do is pick which part of them to fail first.

that is a cruelly intelligent pitch.

which means he or whoever sent him has done their homework.

"if i say yes," she asks, "what exactly am i saying yes to?"

"training first. evaluation. deployment if you pass."

"and if i don't?"

"then you finish your shift, go home, and this conversation never happened."

she almost asks if he's serious.

she doesn't bother. people like him are always serious. that's the problem.

a monitor alarms behind her. room twelve. some fresh disaster. a nurse swears under her breath. another voice calls her name from farther down the hall, wanting something signed, approved, witnessed, solved.

parisa stays where she is.

"and if i say no now," she asks, "do you come back?"

"no."

that, weirdly, feels like the first respectful thing about him.

no pressure campaign. no persuasion. no pretending she matters more to the machine than she actually does.

one offer.

one decision.

she knows this is what makes it dangerous.

there is dignity in being treated like a blade instead of a fool. people like him know that. they know exactly what kinds of people respond to being recognized cleanly, stripped of sentiment.

useful.

not special.

not chosen.

useful.

it is almost insulting how well that works on her.

parisa exhales through her nose and thinks about every time she has stood in the middle of a bad room wishing she had gotten there ten minutes sooner. every time the damage was already too advanced by the time it reached her hands. every time she recognized that medicine is, at its core, an endless negotiation with aftermath.

she hates aftermath.

she hates it so much that for one treacherous second, this feels less like recruitment and more like relief.

"how long do i have?" she asks.

"twenty-four hours."

"you assume i'm not impulsive enough to decide now?"

"i assume you distrust anything that looks too much like inevitability."

he is better at this than she wants him to be.

parisa looks at him for a long moment.

then she says, "leave whatever number you're going to leave and get out of my hospital."

this time he does smile, though only with one side of his mouth.

he slides a card onto the counter. plain. matte. no logo.

just a name she knows is false and a number that probably isn't.

"twenty-four hours," he says again.

then he turns and walks away.

no dramatic pause. no final warning. no backward glance.

just gone.

the hallway rushes back in around the absence he leaves behind. monitors. footsteps. distant shouting. somebody laughing too loudly because that's what people do when the alternative is admitting they're scared.

parisa picks up the card.

stares at it.

room twelve alarms again.

she tucks the card into her pocket and goes to work.

she tells herself she will throw it away later.

she tells herself a lot of things that day.

after shift, she sits alone in her car in the parking garage with the engine off and the card balanced on the steering wheel. daylight leaks pale and mean through the concrete openings. somewhere above her, the city is beginning its usual performance of pretending it isn't built on top of suffering.

twenty-four hours.

she should say no.

she should go home, sleep six hours, come back, and keep walking the path she already fought so hard to earn. attending. physician. legitimate. difficult, yes, but real. no shadows attached. no classified language. no disappearing into a system that has learned how to recognize her type on sight.

instead she keeps thinking about that word.

earlier.

by the time she finally starts the car, the decision is not made.

but it is alive.

that night, she doesn't sleep much. not because she is torn between yes and no, but because she already knows herself well enough to understand what the real problem is.

the real problem is that this sounds like work built exactly for the worst parts of her.

the parts that stay calm too fast.

the parts that don't look away.

the parts that hear unstable environments and think finally, something honest.

the next morning, she calls.

not because she trusts the man.

not because she trusts the government.

not because she believes any machine will ever ask for something without eventually deciding it owns the answer.

she calls because she is young enough to still mistake aptitude for destiny.

because she has spent years saving people from the aftermath and is vain enough, angry enough, hopeful enough to think she might do better if she gets there before the wreckage.

because somewhere deep down, under all the discipline and the bone-deep mistrust, there is still a version of parisa who wants to believe usefulness can be turned into purpose if you hand it to the right people.

the line clicks. a voice answers. no greeting.

just: "doctor farzan."

and parisa, already half-regretting it, says, "tell me where to be."

later, she will understand exactly what kind of offer she accepted.

later, she will recognize the shape of that recruitment for what it really was: not salvation, not patriotism, not even ambition.

just the first institution that looked at her clearly and said yes, you are dangerous in exactly the right way.

and god help her, at the time, that sounded like belonging.
